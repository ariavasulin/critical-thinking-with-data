# HCE Audit — Module 3

Module 3 formalizes the full four-question HCE protocol as a reusable habit. In this audit, students apply the same questions twice: first to Du Bois's original Plate 25, then to their own counter-visualization.

## Purpose

The point of this audit is not to reward agreement with one interpretation. The point is to evaluate whether students can:

- use HCE concepts accurately,
- ground claims in concrete evidence from notebook output and chart design, and
- compare two representations of related data with precision.

## What students submit

Students submit one response with three parts:

1. **Part A (Du Bois plate):** Four short responses (Classification, Performativity, Representation, Agency) applied to Plate 25.
2. **Part B (student chart):** Four short responses (same four concepts) applied to the student's own counter-visualization.
3. **Part C (comparison):** One synthesis paragraph comparing what changes between Part A and Part B and why.

## Required evidence rule

Each response must cite specific evidence. Minimum expectation:

- one visible chart feature (axis, scale, color, annotation, ordering, form),
- one notebook artifact (table, value, code output, or generated figure),
- one explicit interpretive claim connecting the evidence to the HCE concept.

Claims without evidence do not earn above developing-level scores.

## Audit prompts

For **Part A** and **Part B**, answer each question in 2-4 sentences:

1. **Classification**  
   What categories does this representation use? Who decided the categories? What gets counted, and what is absent or invisible?
2. **Performativity**  
   What does this representation set out to describe? What does it bring into being through the act of describing?
3. **Representation**  
   How does this representation "stand for" the people in it? Whose work went into the representation? What does the representation do that the people couldn't?
4. **Agency**  
   Who produced this representation? Whose agency does it amplify, and whose does it constrain?

For **Part C** (5-8 sentences):

Compare your audit of Du Bois's plate (Part A) to the audit of your own counter-visualization (Part B). What stayed the same? What changed? What did you inherit from Du Bois, and what did you deliberately change? What ethical tradeoff is introduced by your design choice?

## Scoring rubric

Each criterion is scored on a 1-4 scale.

### Part A (Du Bois) — four scores

- **4 (Exemplary):** Concept is accurate, evidence is specific and well integrated, claim is nuanced and historically grounded.
- **3 (Proficient):** Concept is accurate, evidence is specific, claim is clear but less nuanced.
- **2 (Developing):** Concept use is partial or generic, evidence is vague or weakly tied to claim.
- **1 (Beginning):** Concept is missing/misused; response is opinion-only or unsupported summary.

### Part B (Student chart) — four scores

Same 1-4 scale, with additional emphasis on whether the student can critically evaluate their own choices rather than merely defend them.

### Part C (Comparison) — one score

- **4:** Precise comparison with explicit tradeoff analysis and concrete cross-reference to Parts A and B evidence.
- **3:** Clear comparison with at least one specific tradeoff.
- **2:** General comparison; tradeoff is implied or underspecified.
- **1:** Minimal or unsupported comparison.

## Suggested weighting

- Part A (4 questions): **40%**
- Part B (4 questions): **40%**
- Part C (comparison): **20%**

## Teacher facilitation notes

- Require students to identify audience explicitly in both Part A and Part B.
- Push beyond taste language ("looks better") toward argument language ("foregrounds X while obscuring Y").
- During feedback, ask students to revise one sentence by adding concrete evidence.
- Use one anonymized exemplar and one near-miss response to calibrate expectations before submission.

## Common pitfalls to flag

- Treating aesthetics as separate from argument.
- Using HCE terms as labels without analysis.
- Describing what is present without naming what is absent.
- Confusing "community-produced" with "bias-free."

## Submission checklist for students

- I answered all four questions for Part A and Part B.
- I cited concrete evidence in every response.
- I completed Part C comparison with one explicit ethical tradeoff.
- My writing names at least one design choice and its consequence.
