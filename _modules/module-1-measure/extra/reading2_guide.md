# Reading Guide 2: "Teaching the Difficult Past of Statistics"

## Primary Source

- **Authors:** Greenwood, C. J., Bhavnani, R., & Harmon, K.
- **Publication:** Journal of Statistics and Data Science Education, 2023
- **URL:** https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/26939169.2023.2224407

### Access Note

If the article is behind a paywall, use this alternative, it covers the same ground and is fully open access:

- **Author:** Aubrey Clayton
- **Title:** "The 'Correlation' Between Statistics and Eugenics"
- **Publication:** Los Angeles Review of Books, 2024
- **URL:** https://lareviewofbooks.org/article/the-correlation-between-statistics-and-eugenics/

Either reading works for the questions below. If you read both, note where they agree and where they emphasize different things.

---

## Why This Reading

Unlike Reading 1, which was written for a general audience, this is a peer-reviewed article published in an academic journal specifically for statistics educators. Its central concern is not just what happened historically but what teachers and students should do about it, how to teach statistics when its foundational methods were invented to justify racial hierarchy. That makes it directly relevant to you: you are sitting in a class right now where this is being taught, which means the questions this article raises are not abstract. The article also draws on examples from real classrooms, so you will see what students and instructors have actually said and done when confronted with this history. A key move the authors make is modeling critical historical thinking, they do not just say "the history was bad" and move on. They try to hold two things at once: these methods are analytically powerful tools, and they were created to cause harm.

---

## Pre-Reading Warm-Up

Answer these two questions in your notebook before reading.

1. If a method is useful and works well, if it produces accurate results, does it matter who invented it or why they invented it? Try to take a position rather than just listing considerations on both sides.

2. Can you think of other technologies, systems, or institutions that were created with harmful or discriminatory intent but are now used in ways that seem beneficial or neutral? What happened to the origins? Did people forget them, actively suppress them, or simply decide they did not matter? Does that history change how you think about those things now?

---

## Guided Reading Questions

Answer these as you read. For question 4 especially, try to think past your first instinct.

1. The article notes that Galton, Pearson, and Fisher are still honored in the names of statistical tests, academic buildings, and professional awards. Does naming matter? When we call something "Pearson's r" or the "Fisher exact test," are we doing anything beyond convenience, or does the name carry something of the ideology of the person attached to it? Can a method carry the ideology of its inventor even after everyone has forgotten the inventor?

2. The article mentions statisticians and scientists in the early twentieth century who challenged eugenicist science on empirical and ethical grounds, people who pushed back at the time, not only in retrospect. Why do you think those critics are less famous today than Galton and Pearson? What does it tell us about whose work gets remembered, preserved, and taught when we answer that question honestly?

3. How does the article suggest instructors should handle the history of eugenics when teaching statistics? What approaches do the authors recommend, and which ones do you think would actually work in a class like this one? Is there anything they recommend that you think would not work, or something they do not mention that you think should be part of the conversation?

4. The article draws a distinction between the statistical methods themselves (tools that are analytically useful) and the ideological purposes those methods were built to serve (which caused enormous harm, including forced sterilization programs, immigration restrictions, and scientific justification for genocide). Is that distinction clean? Where does it hold up, and where does it break down? Is there any case where the design of the tool itself, not just its application, encodes the ideology?

---

## Class Discussion Question

This article was published in 2023, meaning this debate is actively happening right now among professional statisticians, data scientists, and educators, not just historians. The field is arguing about its own past while you are learning the field.

**What responsibility do students of data science have in this conversation?** You are not yet professionals. You did not invent these methods. You did not name the buildings. What, if anything, do you owe to this history, and to the people harmed by the programs these methods were designed to support?

There is no clean answer. The goal is to take the question seriously.

## Connecting the Two Readings

After you have read both Reading 1 (Clayton, Nautilus) and this article, consider:

- Both pieces argue that the history matters. Do they agree on why it matters, or do they emphasize different reasons?
- Reading 1 focuses on the past and its legacy. This article focuses on the present and on pedagogy. Which framing do you find more useful for thinking about what to do?
- Is there anything one article says that the other contradicts, or that the other would challenge?

You do not need to write formal answers to these questions, but bring your thoughts to class.

## A Note on What "Peer-Reviewed" Means

This reading comes from an academic journal, which means it went through peer review before publication: other scholars in the field read the draft anonymously and evaluated whether the argument was rigorous, the evidence was sound, and the conclusions followed from the evidence. That does not mean the article is correct or that you must agree with it. It means the claims have been vetted to a standard higher than general journalism. Read it with the same critical attention you would give any source, agree where the argument earns your agreement, push back where it does not.
