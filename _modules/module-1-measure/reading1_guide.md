# Reading Guide 1: "How Eugenics Shaped Statistics"

## Source Information

- **Author:** Aubrey Clayton
- **Publication:** Nautilus, October 2020
- **URL:** https://nautil.us/how-eugenics-shaped-statistics-238014/
- **Length:** Approximately 3,500 words — freely available online
- **Suggested reading time:** 45-60 minutes of careful, annotated reading

---

## Why This Reading

This article is one of the clearest accounts available — written for a general audience, not specialists — of how the founders of modern statistics built their scientific innovations directly on top of a eugenic ideology. Aubrey Clayton traces a line from Francis Galton through Karl Pearson and Ronald Fisher, showing not just that these men held racist beliefs as a personal matter, but that their scientific programs and their statistical programs were the same project. One of the most striking details Clayton offers is the word "homogeneous": he shows how the same vocabulary traveled between statistical papers and racial ideology, carrying meaning in both directions. The article also engages directly with a contemporary debate — the recent removal of Galton's and Pearson's names from buildings at University College London — and asks what it would mean for the field of statistics to genuinely reckon with this history.

## Guided Reading Questions

Read carefully and answer these questions as you go. Quote directly from the article at least once in your responses to questions 2 and 3.

1. The article argues that statistics is long overdue for "decolonization." What does that word mean in this context — what would it actually involve for a scientific field to be decolonized? Do you agree with Clayton's argument that statistics needs this? What would you need to see to be convinced either way?

2. The author describes Karl Pearson using the word "homogeneous" in both his statistical writing and his explicitly racial writing. What does it mean that the same vocabulary served both purposes simultaneously? Is that a coincidence of language, or does it tell us something more important about how scientific concepts and political ideology interact?

3. According to the article, what concrete harms in criminal justice and education are still being caused today by the eugenicist legacy within statistics? Be specific — name the methods or practices Clayton points to. How do those harms connect to the original ideological purposes those methods were designed to serve?

4. The statistician Nathaniel Joselson is quoted saying that "objectivity is extremely overrated." What does he mean by that? Is he saying data is useless, or is he making a more specific point about the limits of claiming objectivity? Do you agree or disagree with him — and what would you need to argue either position convincingly?

5. The article ends with an open question: how should we teach statistical methods that were invented by eugenicists? Clayton raises it but does not fully answer it. What is your answer? Consider: is there a difference between using a tool and endorsing its origins? Does it matter whether students know the history? Is there something lost if we simply strip the history away and teach the math as neutral?

## Vocabulary to Look Up Before Reading

Look these up before you start reading — having working definitions will make the article significantly easier to follow.

- **Eugenics** — what it means, where the word comes from, and when the movement peaked
- **Correlation coefficient** — what it measures, and what a value between -1 and +1 means
- **Regression** — in the statistical sense, not the psychological sense
- **Heritability** — how geneticists define this term, and what it does and does not claim
- **Determinism** — in the philosophical sense, as applied to human behavior and social outcomes
