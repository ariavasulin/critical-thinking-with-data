# HCE Audit — Module 1

Module 1 introduces the HCE protocol as a structured habit. This first audit is intentionally scaffolded: students receive vocabulary definitions, a worked example, and only two of the four HCE questions. The goal is to build confidence and precision before the more demanding audits in Modules 2 and 3.

## Purpose

The point of this audit is not to reward agreement with one interpretation. The point is to evaluate whether students can:

- use the HCE concepts of **Classification** and **Performativity** accurately,
- ground claims in concrete evidence from the Galton dataset and their notebook output, and
- distinguish between what a dataset measures and what it cannot represent.

## What students submit

Students submit one document with two parts:

1. **Classification** — one short response (2–4 sentences) applying the concept of Classification to the Galton dataset.
2. **Performativity** — one short response (2–4 sentences) applying the concept of Performativity to the Galton dataset, with specific attention to the 1.08 transmutation.

## Scaffolding provided

Because this is the first audit, teachers should distribute the following with the prompt:

- **Vocabulary sheet:** One-paragraph definition of Classification and Performativity, drawn from the HCE Toolkit.
- **Worked example:** A sample 3-sentence response applying Classification to a different dataset (not Galton), so students see the expected format without being given the answer.

## Required evidence rule

Each response must cite specific evidence. Minimum expectation:

- one concrete data feature (column name, value range, sample size, gender breakdown, or absent variable),
- one notebook artifact (chart, `.describe()` output, regression slope, or transmutation comparison figure),
- one explicit interpretive claim connecting the evidence to the HCE concept.

Claims without evidence do not earn above developing-level scores.

## Audit prompts

Answer each question in 2–4 sentences:

1. **Classification**
   What categories structure the Galton dataset? Who set those categories? What is counted, and what is absent? Consider: which populations are included in the sample, which are excluded, and how the column structure itself encodes assumptions about what matters.

2. **Performativity**
   Does the Galton dataset merely describe height, or does it bring something into being by describing in this way? Focus on the 1.08 transmutation: what claim does multiplying female heights by 1.08 perform? What happens to the regression line when you toggle the multiplier on and off — and what does that tell you about the relationship between a methodological choice and a conclusion?

## Scoring rubric

Each criterion is scored on a 1–4 scale.

### Classification — one score

- **4 (Exemplary):** Concept is accurate, evidence is specific and well integrated, claim names both what is present *and* what is structurally absent from the dataset.
- **3 (Proficient):** Concept is accurate, evidence is specific, claim is clear but addresses only presence or absence, not both.
- **2 (Developing):** Concept use is partial or generic, evidence is vague or weakly tied to claim.
- **1 (Beginning):** Concept is missing/misused; response is opinion-only or unsupported summary.

### Performativity — one score

- **4 (Exemplary):** Concept is accurate, response connects the 1.08 transmutation to a specific shift in the regression output, and articulates what the multiplier *performs* (not just what it does mechanically).
- **3 (Proficient):** Concept is accurate, evidence is specific, but the connection between the methodological choice and its performative effect is underdeveloped.
- **2 (Developing):** Concept use is partial, or the response treats 1.08 as a neutral correction without interrogating it.
- **1 (Beginning):** Concept is missing/misused; response does not engage with the transmutation evidence.

## Suggested weighting

- Classification: **50%**
- Performativity: **50%**

## Teacher facilitation notes

- Distribute the vocabulary sheet and worked example *before* students begin writing.
- Circulate during writing time and ask students to point to specific notebook evidence before they draft sentences.
- Push beyond taste language ("the data is biased") toward structural language ("the dataset classifies by gender but not by race, which means ___").
- Use one anonymized exemplar and one near-miss response to calibrate expectations before submission.
- Remind students that this is the first of three audits — the protocol will grow in scope and rigor.

## Common pitfalls to flag

- Saying "the data is biased" without naming *which* bias or *who* is affected.
- Using HCE terms as labels without analysis (e.g., writing "this is Classification" without explaining what is classified and what is left out).
- Treating the 1.08 multiplier as a neutral scientific adjustment rather than a methodological choice with consequences.
- Confusing "the dataset is old" with a structural critique — age is not the issue; who was included and what was measured is.

## Submission checklist for students

- I answered both the Classification and Performativity questions.
- I cited at least one specific data feature and one notebook artifact in each response.
- Each response includes an explicit interpretive claim, not just a description.
- My Classification response names what is *absent* from the dataset, not just what is present.
- My Performativity response references the 1.08 transmutation and its effect on the regression line.
