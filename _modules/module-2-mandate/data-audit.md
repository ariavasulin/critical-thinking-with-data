# Data Audit — Module 2

Module 2 extends the HCE protocol from two questions to a three-part analysis. Students apply HCE concepts to California sterilization data and produce a structured cross-module synthesis linking Module 1 (Galton) and Module 2 (state policy data).

## Purpose

The point of this audit is not to reward agreement with one interpretation. The point is to evaluate whether students can:

- use HCE concepts accurately,
- ground claims in concrete evidence from notebook output and historical context, and
- compare two datasets across modules with conceptual precision.

## What students submit

Students submit one response with three parts:

1. **Classification (Module 2 dataset):** One short response (2-4 sentences) identifying how categories are set in the sterilization data and what they exclude.
2. **Representation (Module 2 dataset):** One short response (2-4 sentences) analyzing how institutional records stand in for people and lived experience.
3. **Cross-module synthesis (Modules 1 + 2):** One paragraph (5-8 sentences) comparing the Galton dataset and the sterilization dataset using **Classification** and **Performativity**.

## Required evidence rule

Each response must cite specific evidence. Minimum expectation:

- one concrete data feature (column, count, ratio, proxy rule, or missing field),
- one notebook artifact (contingency table, rate ratio, chi-squared output, sensitivity analysis result, chart, or diagnosis table),
- one explicit interpretive claim connecting the evidence to the HCE concept.

Claims without evidence do not earn above developing-level scores.

## Audit prompts

Answer each question in 2-4 sentences unless otherwise noted:

1. **Classification**  
   What categories structure the sterilization dataset? Who set those categories, and for what institutional purpose? What is counted, and what is absent (for example, race as a direct variable)?

2. **Representation**  
   How do these records stand in for people? What do aggregate counts reveal, and what do they flatten or erase about individual experience, coercion, and survival?

3. **Cross-module synthesis (5-8 sentences)**  
   Compare Module 1 (Galton heights) and Module 2 (California sterilization data) using **Classification** and **Performativity**.  
   - How do category choices differ between the two datasets?  
   - How does each dataset do more than describe (what does each one *perform*)?  
   - What is one key ethical similarity and one key ethical difference across the modules?

## Scoring rubric

Each criterion is scored on a 1-4 scale.

### Classification (Module 2) — one score

- **4 (Exemplary):** Concept is accurate, evidence is specific and well integrated, response identifies both what is present and what is structurally absent.
- **3 (Proficient):** Concept is accurate, evidence is specific, claim is clear but less complete on presence/absence.
- **2 (Developing):** Concept use is partial or generic, evidence is vague or weakly tied to claim.
- **1 (Beginning):** Concept is missing/misused; response is opinion-only or unsupported summary.

### Representation (Module 2) — one score

- **4 (Exemplary):** Concept is accurate, response clearly distinguishes aggregate pattern from lived experience, and evidence is concrete.
- **3 (Proficient):** Concept is accurate and evidence is specific, but analysis of what is erased is underdeveloped.
- **2 (Developing):** Concept use is partial, and/or response treats data as fully transparent to reality.
- **1 (Beginning):** Concept is missing/misused; response does not engage representational limits.

### Cross-module synthesis — one score

- **4 (Exemplary):** Precise comparison across both modules with explicit use of Classification and Performativity, supported by evidence from each notebook.
- **3 (Proficient):** Clear comparison using both concepts with at least one specific piece of evidence from each module.
- **2 (Developing):** General or one-sided comparison; concepts are named but weakly applied.
- **1 (Beginning):** Minimal or unsupported comparison.

## Suggested weighting

- Classification: **30%**
- Representation: **30%**
- Cross-module synthesis: **40%**

## Teacher facilitation notes

- Remove the worked example used in Module 1; students should now apply the concepts more independently.
- During writing time, ask students to point to exact notebook evidence before drafting claims.
- Require at least one concrete reference from Module 1 and one from Module 2 in the synthesis paragraph.
- Push beyond generic language ("this is biased") toward structural language ("race is proxied by Spanish surname, which means ___").
- Use one anonymized exemplar and one near-miss response to calibrate expectations before submission.

## Common pitfalls to flag

- Treating proxy uncertainty as equivalent to invalid data rather than bounded uncertainty.
- Confusing statistical significance with effect size or causal explanation.
- Using HCE terms as labels without analysis.
- Describing historical harm without connecting to the dataset's structure.
- Writing synthesis as two separate summaries instead of a direct comparison.

## Submission checklist for students

- I answered all three parts (Classification, Representation, and synthesis).
- I cited concrete evidence from the Module 2 notebook in both short responses.
- My synthesis cites evidence from both Module 1 and Module 2.
- I used both Classification and Performativity explicitly in the synthesis.
- I made at least one comparative claim (similarity or difference) with evidence.
